# Chapter 3: Literature Review and State of the Art

This chapter surveys the evolution of human action recognition, tracing the field from the hand-crafted spatiotemporal descriptors that defined its first two decades through the four families of deep architectures that this thesis compares directly — two-dimensional convolutional networks, recurrent networks, three-dimensional convolutional networks, and Transformers — and finally through the benchmark datasets and comparative studies that have shaped their development. The presentation is organised chronologically within each paradigm so that every section not only summarises its representative methods and their reported results, but also makes explicit the specific limitation that motivated the paradigm which followed, building the argument that culminates in the controlled four-way comparison at the heart of this work.

## 3.1 Early Approaches to Action Recognition

The problem of human action recognition from video has occupied computer vision researchers for several decades, dating back to the early benchmarks of Schuldt et al. [51] and Blank et al. [52] in 2004–2005, which framed recognition as the classification of short, trimmed clips into a small set of action categories. Prior to the deep learning era, the dominant paradigm relied on hand-crafted feature descriptors designed to capture motion patterns, appearance statistics, and spatiotemporal structure from raw pixel data, which were then aggregated into a fixed-length video representation and fed to a shallow classifier such as a support vector machine. These methods were elegant in their motivation and, in their most refined form, achieved respectable accuracy: improved trajectories with Fisher Vector encoding reached roughly 85.9% on UCF101 and 57.2% on HMDB51 [4], figures that would not be decisively surpassed until the maturation of deep video networks. Nevertheless, they ultimately struggled to generalise across the full diversity of real-world action classes and recording conditions, because every stage of the pipeline encoded a fixed human prior rather than a representation adapted to the data.

### 3.1.1 Local Spatio-Temporal Descriptors

One of the foundational contributions to action recognition came from the extension of image-domain histogram descriptors to the temporal domain. Dalal and Triggs introduced the Histogram of Oriented Gradients (HOG) [1] in 2005 for pedestrian detection in still images, demonstrating that dense, locally-normalised gradient orientation histograms — computed over small cells, grouped into overlapping blocks, and contrast-normalised — could robustly encode shape and appearance information while remaining largely invariant to illumination and small geometric deformation. The extension of this idea to video was conceptually straightforward: by computing oriented histograms not of spatial intensity gradients but of the optical flow field, Histogram of Optical Flow (HOF) descriptors could encode local motion direction and magnitude as a complement to appearance. Motion Boundary Histograms (MBH), introduced by Dalal et al. [2] in 2006, went further by computing gradient histograms of the two optical-flow components separately rather than of the raw flow values. Because spatial differentiation of the flow field removes locally constant motion, MBH suppressed the smooth background motion induced by camera translation while emphasising motion boundaries where the body moved relative to its surroundings — a critical advantage for recognition in cluttered, non-static scenes, and a property that made MBH the single most discriminative of the three descriptor families.

The most influential hand-crafted approach of the pre-deep-learning era was the Dense Trajectories framework introduced by Wang et al. [3] in 2011. Rather than extracting features only at sparse interest points, Dense Trajectories sampled feature points on a dense grid across multiple spatial scales and tracked each point through a fixed temporal window (typically fifteen frames) by following the dense optical flow field, discarding tracks in homogeneous regions where tracking is unreliable. For each resulting trajectory, multiple descriptors were computed within a space-time volume aligned to the path: HOG to capture static appearance, HOF to capture absolute motion, and MBH to capture relative motion, together with the normalised trajectory shape itself. This combination produced a rich, complementary description of both the static appearance and the dynamic motion along each track, and substantially outperformed the sparse interest-point pipelines that preceded it. The Improved Dense Trajectories (iDT) of Wang and Schmid [4] in 2013 addressed the principal remaining weakness: the conflation of camera motion with genuine body motion. By matching feature points between consecutive frames using SURF descriptors and optical flow and estimating a background homography with RANSAC, iDT could cancel the global camera-induced flow before computing the motion descriptors, while a human detector masked out person regions so that they did not corrupt the homography estimate. This camera-motion compensation removed spurious trajectories generated by panning and shake and rectified the flow used for HOF and MBH, yielding consistent improvements on every benchmark of the time. Encoded with Fisher Vectors, iDT defined the pre-deep-learning state of the art — approximately 85.9% on UCF101 and 57.2% on HMDB51 [4] — and remained competitive well into the deep learning era, to the extent that early deep models were frequently reported in combination with iDT rather than as outright replacements for it.

### 3.1.2 Interest Point Detectors and Global Encodings

Before dense sampling became standard, the field concentrated on detecting a sparse set of salient spatiotemporal locations. Laptev [5] introduced Space-Time Interest Points (STIPs) in 2005, extending the Harris corner detector to the spatiotemporal domain by forming a 3D second-moment matrix from spatial and temporal image derivatives and seeking points where the signal varied significantly along all three axes simultaneously. STIPs provided a sparse, computationally efficient alternative to dense processing and localised events such as the abrupt reversals of limb motion, but they fired only rarely, were sensitive to the choice of spatial and temporal scales, and struggled with smooth, periodic, or low-contrast motions such as walking observed at a distance. Dollar et al. [6], also in 2005, proposed the Cuboid detector as a more responsive alternative, applying a 2D Gaussian smoothing kernel in space and a quadrature pair of 1D Gabor filters in time and selecting local maxima of the resulting response; this separable design produced far denser detections than STIPs on periodic motion and extracted small space-time volumes ("cuboids") around each maximum for description. These detectors established the interest-point-plus-descriptor template that dense trajectories would later generalise by simply sampling everywhere.

Following the success of Bag-of-Visual-Words (BoVW) models in image categorisation [7], action recognition researchers adopted a closely analogous pipeline: local descriptors were quantised against a codebook of visual words learned by k-means clustering, and each video was represented by a normalised histogram counting how many descriptors were assigned to each word. While straightforward to implement and compact, hard assignment of every descriptor to its single nearest centroid discarded the descriptor's position within its Voronoi cell and therefore much of the information it carried, so accuracy saturated as the vocabulary grew. Fisher Vectors [8], introduced by Perronnin et al. in 2010 and derived from the Fisher kernel of a Gaussian Mixture Model fitted to the descriptor space, provided a principled improvement by encoding the gradient of the log-likelihood with respect to the mixture parameters — capturing both the first-order (mean) and second-order (variance) deviations of the assigned descriptors from each Gaussian component rather than a mere count. This soft, higher-order encoding produced a representation that was far more informative per visual word, so Fisher Vectors consistently outperformed BoVW at equivalent codebook sizes while permitting much smaller codebooks for a given accuracy; the resulting high-dimensional vectors became the de-facto encoding strategy for iDT [4] and the immediate baseline that the first deep video models had to surpass.

### 3.1.3 Limitations Motivating Deep Learning

Despite their ingenuity, hand-crafted approaches suffered from several fundamental limitations that ultimately motivated the transition to learned representations. First, the multi-stage pipeline — detection, description, encoding, classification — was brittle and disjoint: each stage was optimised in isolation, no error signal flowed back from the classifier to the descriptor, and a failure at any stage propagated unrecoverably to the next. Second, the front end was computationally heavy, dominated by per-frame optical flow that had to be solved with iterative methods such as Farneback's polynomial-expansion estimator [9] or the variational TV-L1 algorithm [10], and compounded by the very high dimensionality of Fisher Vectors, which made both storage and SVM training costly at scale. Third, the representational capacity of a fixed vocabulary was inherently bounded; rare or compositional actions that did not decompose neatly into the standard motion primitives captured by the codebook were poorly represented, and the descriptors themselves — HOG, HOF, MBH — were frozen choices that could not specialise to a dataset. Fourth, and most critically, every feature was hand-designed from human intuition about which motion patterns matter, with no mechanism to learn the representation from the statistical regularities of the training data, so improvements demanded further manual engineering rather than simply more data or more capacity. These limitations set the stage for the deep learning revolution in visual recognition, which first transformed the image domain — where learned hierarchical features decisively displaced hand-crafted descriptors — before being extended to video.

## 3.2 Convolutional Neural Networks for Image Classification

The development of deep CNNs for image classification is among the most consequential intellectual histories in modern computer science, not only because of its intrinsic importance but because every major video recognition architecture discussed in this thesis is directly descended from the image CNN lineage. The two-stream networks of Section 3.3 reuse image CNNs verbatim on individual frames and flow stacks; the 3D CNNs of Section 3.5 inflate or factorise the same convolutional blocks into the temporal dimension; and even the video transformers of later sections inherit, through the Vision Transformer, the patch-based tokenisation and large-scale pretraining mindset that the CNN era established. Understanding the image-domain progression from LeNet to ResNet is therefore a prerequisite for understanding the video models that follow.

### 3.2.1 Foundations: LeNet to AlexNet

LeCun et al. [11] demonstrated in 1998 that convolutional networks trained end-to-end with backpropagation could outperform hand-engineered systems on handwritten digit recognition, deploying their model at industrial scale to read the digits on bank cheques. LeNet-5, with its alternating convolution and subsampling (pooling) layers feeding a small fully-connected classifier and roughly sixty thousand learnable parameters, established the architectural blueprint — local receptive fields, shared weights, and progressive spatial downsampling with increasing channel depth — that would define the field for two decades. However, scaling this blueprint from 32×32 greyscale digits to the clutter and variability of natural images was impeded for years by three coupled obstacles: the computational cost of convolution on the hardware of the era, the absence of sufficiently large annotated datasets, and the difficulty of optimising deep networks built from saturating sigmoid or tanh activations, whose gradients vanished as signals propagated backward through many layers.

The breakthrough came in 2012, when Krizhevsky, Sutskever, and Hinton introduced AlexNet [12] and won the ImageNet Large Scale Visual Recognition Challenge (ILSVRC) [13] with a top-5 error of 15.3% — more than ten percentage points below the second-place entry at roughly 26.2%, a margin so large that it was widely read as a paradigm shift rather than an incremental gain. AlexNet's success rested on several concurrent advances brought together at the right scale: an eight-layer network with roughly sixty million parameters, trained across two GTX 580 GPUs whose limited memory forced a now-iconic split of the network into two parallel streams; Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU) activations [14], whose non-saturating gradient accelerated convergence by several times relative to tanh and made training such depth feasible; aggressive data augmentation and local response normalisation to combat overfitting on the 1.2-million-image training set; and dropout regularisation [15] in the fully-connected layers, which prevented the co-adaptation of neurons by randomly zeroing activations during training. The sheer magnitude of AlexNet's improvement over the best hand-crafted pipelines was sufficient to redirect the entire field of computer vision toward learned deep features, and it directly catalysed the application of the same ideas to video within the following two years.

### 3.2.2 Depth and Architecture Refinements

Simonyan and Zisserman's VGGNet [16] established that network depth, rather than large convolutional receptive fields, was the principal driver of performance. By replacing the large 7×7 and 11×11 filters of AlexNet with stacks of small 3×3 convolutions — a stack of two such layers spans the same 5×5 receptive field, and three spans 7×7, but with fewer parameters and two or three non-linearities interposed instead of one — VGGNet pushed depth to 16 and 19 weight layers and reduced ILSVRC top-5 error to roughly 7.3%. The price of this homogeneous design was scale: VGG-16 carries approximately 138 million parameters, the great majority of them in its three large fully-connected layers, making it memory-hungry and slow at inference. Crucially, however, VGGNet demonstrated systematic scalability, with deeper configurations consistently outperforming shallower ones provided optimisation could be made to converge, and the simplicity and regularity of its design made it a favoured backbone for feature extraction in many subsequent video systems, including the two-stream networks discussed in Section 3.3.

Szegedy et al.'s GoogLeNet / Inception-v1 architecture [17] pursued a complementary objective: rather than maximising depth alone, it maximised computational efficiency through the Inception module, which applied convolutions of several kernel sizes (1×1, 3×3, 5×5) together with a pooling branch in parallel and concatenated their feature maps so that the network could capture structure at multiple scales within a single layer. The decisive trick was the use of 1×1 convolutions as dimensionality-reducing bottlenecks placed before the expensive 3×3 and 5×5 branches, which sharply curtailed the channel count entering those branches. As a result, GoogLeNet reached a top-5 error of 6.67% — winning ILSVRC 2014 ahead of VGGNet — across 22 layers while using only on the order of five to seven million parameters, roughly an order of magnitude fewer than AlexNet and some twenty times fewer than VGG-16, a striking demonstration that accuracy and parameter economy were not in conflict. Ioffe and Szegedy [18] subsequently introduced Batch Normalisation (BN) in 2015, which standardises the inputs to each layer to zero mean and unit variance using statistics computed across the mini-batch, followed by a learned scale and shift. By stabilising the distribution of layer inputs during training, BN permitted markedly higher learning rates, greatly reduced sensitivity to weight initialisation, accelerated convergence, and supplied a mild regularising effect that often lessened the need for dropout. Batch normalisation became a near-universal component of essentially all subsequent architectures and was indispensable to the stable training of the very deep 3D CNNs discussed in Section 3.5.

### 3.2.3 Residual Networks and the Backbone of This Thesis

The most significant architectural contribution of the mid-2010s was the Residual Network (ResNet) of He et al. [65], which won ILSVRC 2015 with a top-5 error of 3.57% using a 152-layer network — for the first time surpassing estimated human-level performance on the benchmark and reaching a depth roughly eight times that of VGGNet. The central observation motivating ResNet was the degradation problem: beyond a certain depth, adding further layers caused training accuracy — not merely test accuracy — to deteriorate, which rules out overfitting and instead points to an optimisation failure, since a deeper network can in principle represent any shallower one by setting its extra layers to the identity. He et al. resolved this by reformulating each block to learn a residual: rather than fitting a desired underlying mapping H(x) directly, a block fits F(x) = H(x) − x and adds the input back through a parameter-free shortcut connection, so that the identity mapping becomes trivially representable as F(x) = 0 and the optimiser need only learn the deviation from identity. These shortcut connections also create direct paths along which gradients can flow during backpropagation, bypassing the intervening non-linear layers and largely preventing the gradient attenuation that had previously made very deep networks untrainable. ResNet-50, the 50-layer variant built from three-layer bottleneck blocks (1×1 reduce, 3×3, 1×1 expand), strikes an excellent balance between representational capacity and computational cost, comprising approximately 25.6 million parameters and attaining around 76% top-1 accuracy on ImageNet; it is employed in this thesis as the 2D-CNN backbone both for the Temporal Segment Network baseline (Section 3.3.2) and for the CNN-LSTM recurrent model. Its pre-trained ImageNet weights serve as the initialisation for these video models, leveraging the transfer learning paradigm discussed below.

### 3.2.4 Transfer Learning and Fine-Tuning

Transfer learning [19] — initialising a model with weights pre-trained on a large source dataset and then fine-tuning on a smaller target dataset — became a cornerstone of applied deep learning for visual recognition, precisely because the target datasets of greatest interest are typically far too small to train a high-capacity network from random initialisation. Yosinski et al. [20] provided systematic empirical evidence for why it works: by transferring and freezing successively deeper layers of a pre-trained network, they showed that early convolutional layers learn generic, broadly transferable features — oriented edges, colour blobs, and textures that recur across virtually all natural images — while later layers progressively specialise to the semantics of the source task, so transfer is most beneficial when the source features are reused and adapted rather than relearned. In the action recognition setting this matters acutely: fine-tuning a model pre-trained on the 1.2-million-image ImageNet dataset consistently and substantially outperformed training from scratch on datasets such as UCF101 (around 13,000 clips over 101 classes [54]) and HMDB51 (roughly 7,000 clips over 51 classes [53]), where the limited data would otherwise drive severe overfitting. This paradigm is exploited extensively throughout this thesis: the ResNet-50 backbone, the R(2+1)D 3D-CNN, and the VideoMAE transformer all rely on large-dataset pretraining — on ImageNet or on Kinetics — as the foundation upon which fine-tuning to the target action recognition benchmarks is performed.

## 3.3 2D-CNN Approaches for Video Understanding

The ImageNet revolution raised an immediate question for the video recognition community: how should spatial CNNs, which lack an explicit temporal dimension, be adapted to understand motion and temporal dynamics? Having shown decisively on still images that learned hierarchical features outperform the hand-crafted descriptors of Section 3.1, researchers were strongly motivated to transfer the same backbones to video. The obstacle was structural rather than merely empirical: a 2D convolution slides only over the spatial axes of a single frame, so it has no native mechanism for relating pixels across time, whereas the discriminative content of an action — a wave, a throw, a fall — lives precisely in how appearance evolves frame to frame. The first wave of deep video methods therefore largely treated video as a collection of frames or frame-level features and bolted a temporal-aggregation mechanism on top of an image network. They differed chiefly in where motion entered the pipeline (as a separate input modality, as a sampling strategy, or as a learned pooling) and these choices map directly onto the architectures below, which between roughly 2014 and 2018 closed most of the gap to, and then overtook, the improved dense trajectories of [4] that had dominated the pre-deep era.

### 3.3.1 Two-Stream Networks

Simonyan and Zisserman [21] proposed the Two-Stream Network architecture in 2014, which became the dominant video recognition paradigm for several years. The key insight was to decouple spatial appearance modelling from motion modelling by operating two independent CNN streams: a spatial stream processing individual RGB frames, and a temporal stream processing a stack of pre-computed optical flow frames. Each stream was a deep CNN — a VGG-M-style network of the same lineage as the VGGNet family [16] — trained independently; at test time, their softmax scores were fused by averaging or a linear SVM. The temporal stream's input was constructed by stacking the horizontal and vertical components of dense optical flow over a short window of roughly ten consecutive frames, yielding an input volume of about twenty channels that the first convolutional layer consumes much as a still network consumes the three RGB channels. This architectural decision was motivated by the observation that optical flow, computed here with classical variational estimators of the TV-L1 family [10], explicitly encodes inter-frame displacement without requiring the network to infer motion implicitly from appearance changes — a task that is particularly difficult for a 2D CNN whose filters never span the temporal axis. The division of labour also offered a pragmatic data-efficiency benefit: on datasets as small as UCF101 and HMDB51 the spatial stream could be pre-trained on ImageNet and the motion stream learned a representation whose statistics differ enough from natural images that it overfits less to scene context. Two-Stream Networks achieved 88.0% on UCF101 and 59.4% on HMDB51 [21], competitive with iDT [4] at the time and a clear demonstration that deep CNNs could rival hand-crafted approaches even though neither stream performed genuine spatiotemporal convolution; tellingly, the temporal (flow) stream alone outperformed the spatial (RGB) stream on both benchmarks, quantifying just how much of the signal in trimmed action clips is carried by motion rather than static appearance.

However, the Two-Stream framework had significant practical limitations. Optical flow pre-computation was expensive both in time and storage — every clip had to be passed through an external flow estimator and the resulting displacement fields cached to disk, often dwarfing the size of the original video — and this offline dependency sits awkwardly with any claim of an end-to-end learned system. The two streams were trained in isolation and fused only by averaging their class posteriors, preventing the network from learning cross-modal interactions in which appearance disambiguates motion or vice versa. Furthermore, the temporal stream processed only a small, fixed window of flow frames (typically ten), so its effective temporal context spanned a fraction of a second; long-range structure — the ordering of sub-actions across an entire clip — remained outside its receptive field. These three deficiencies, the cost of flow, the absence of joint modelling, and the short temporal horizon, set the agenda for the methods that followed.

### 3.3.2 Temporal Segment Networks

Wang et al. [22] addressed the limited temporal coverage of Two-Stream Networks with the Temporal Segment Network (TSN), which introduced sparse temporal sampling as a principled strategy for capturing long-range temporal dynamics without the prohibitive memory cost of processing all frames. In TSN, each video is divided into K equally-spaced temporal segments — typically K = 3 — and a single frame (or short optical flow stack) is randomly sampled from each segment during training. Each snippet is processed by a shared-weight CNN, and the resulting segment-level predictions are combined by a consensus function, in practice the average of the segment-level class scores prior to the softmax, so that the loss is computed on the video-level consensus and gradients flow back through every sampled snippet. The design rests on the prior that within a trimmed action clip consecutive frames are highly redundant, so a sparse, clip-spanning sample is a lower-variance estimator of the video's content than a dense but temporally local window; crucially, because the K segments tile the whole video regardless of its length, TSN attains full-clip temporal coverage at constant computational cost, directly remedying the short-horizon limitation of the two-stream model. The paper also contributed a set of training practices that proved broadly influential — cross-modality pre-training to initialise the flow network from ImageNet weights, partial batch-normalisation freezing [18] to combat overfitting on small datasets, and aggressive augmentation — which together let deeper backbones be trained on UCF101 without collapse. With a BN-Inception backbone [18] and combined RGB + optical flow streams, TSN reached 94.2% on UCF101 and 69.4% on HMDB51 [22], substantially advancing the state of the art; the RGB-only configuration scores several points lower, again underscoring how much accuracy on these benchmarks still derived from the hand-computed motion modality rather than from learned spatial features alone. The TSN paradigm forms the basis of the 2D-CNN baseline employed in this thesis, using ResNet-50 as the backbone and RGB-only input. The RGB-only choice is deliberate: it isolates the contribution of the spatial stream and the segment-consensus mechanism without the confound — and the substantial preprocessing burden — of optical flow, yielding a clean reference point against which the genuinely spatiotemporal 3D-CNN and Transformer models of the following sections can be compared.

### 3.3.3 ActionVLAD and Temporal Relation Networks

Girdhar et al. [23] proposed ActionVLAD, extending the image-level VLAD (Vector of Locally Aggregated Descriptors) encoding — a close relative of the Fisher-vector pooling [8] used over the hand-crafted features of Section 3.1 — to video by aggregating CNN features across both space and time using a differentiable, learnable assignment. Rather than collapsing the per-frame feature maps by simple temporal averaging, which discards the distribution of local descriptors, ActionVLAD maintains a set of learned cluster centres (an "action vocabulary") and, for every local feature at every spatial location and timestep, accumulates its soft-assigned residual to each centre. The concatenated residuals form a single fixed-length video-level descriptor that summarises which characteristic action primitives appear anywhere in the clip and how strongly, an aggregation that is permutation-invariant over time yet far richer than a mean. Because the assignment is a soft-max over centre distances, the whole module is end-to-end trainable and can be dropped on top of either stream; applied to two-stream features it reached roughly 92.7% on UCF101, confirming that the choice of pooling — not merely the choice of backbone — materially affects accuracy. Its limitation is the mirror image of its strength: by design it is order-agnostic, so it captures the presence of motion primitives but not the temporal grammar that relates them.

Zhou et al. [24] introduced the Temporal Relation Network (TRN), which addressed exactly that gap by explicitly modelling temporal ordering and relations between frames sampled at multiple temporal scales. Given a sparse set of frame-level features, TRN forms ordered tuples — pairs, triples, and higher-order combinations of frames in their natural temporal sequence — and passes each tuple through a small multilayer perceptron whose outputs are summed within a scale and then across scales, so the network learns which orderings of sub-events are discriminative for a given class. This multi-scale relational pooling lets the same architecture reason about both fast, two-frame transitions and slower, multi-frame progressions. The payoff is most visible on the temporally demanding Something-Something dataset [60], whose classes — for example "pushing something from left to right" versus its reverse — are deliberately constructed so that they cannot be separated from appearance or from an unordered bag of frames; TRN's substantial margin there over averaging-based baselines made concrete the claim that temporal order carries information that the pooling strategies of TSN and ActionVLAD systematically throw away, and it foreshadowed the order-sensitive modelling that 3D convolutions and, later, space-time attention would pursue more thoroughly.

### 3.3.4 Limitations of 2D-CNN Approaches

Despite their success, the 2D-CNN approaches surveyed above share a fundamental limitation: the convolutional backbone itself only ever sees one frame at a time, and all temporal reasoning is delegated to a separate aggregation stage — late score fusion in the two-stream model, segment consensus in TSN, residual pooling in ActionVLAD, or a shallow relational MLP in TRN. None of these learns spatiotemporal features jointly within the convolutional hierarchy, so the fine-grained coupling of pose and velocity that unfolds over a handful of adjacent frames is never represented by a single learned operator; it can only be approximated after the fact, from features that were extracted with no knowledge that a temporal axis exists. Optical flow partially compensates by supplying explicit motion as a second input, and indeed the quantitative results above show that the flow stream often contributes more than the RGB stream, but this is precisely the point: the motion that matters is being handed to the network as a fixed, hand-engineered field [10] computed by an external algorithm, reintroducing the very reliance on designed features that deep learning set out to eliminate, and carrying a heavy cost in computation and storage. The conclusion drawn by the field, and the through-line into the remainder of this chapter, was that motion should be learned inside the network rather than supplied to it — a realisation that motivated extending convolution itself into the temporal domain in the three-dimensional architectures of Section 3.5, and in parallel the recurrent sequence models examined next.

## 3.4 Recurrent Neural Networks for Sequence Modelling

Parallel to the CNN-based approaches, a distinct line of work treated action recognition as a sequence modelling problem: given a temporally ordered sequence of frame-level visual features, learn to classify the sequence as a whole. Where the 2D-CNN methods of Section 3.3 aggregated frames with order-agnostic or fixed-window operators, the recurrent view kept time explicit, processing frames one after another and threading a state vector through the sequence so that, in principle, each prediction could depend on the entire history that preceded it. This framing was a natural import from natural language processing and time-series analysis, where Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) were the established tool for variable-length sequential data, and it carried an attractive promise for video: a single mechanism that could in theory model dependencies of arbitrary temporal extent rather than the fixed horizon baked into a convolution's kernel size.

### 3.4.1 Vanilla RNNs and the Vanishing Gradient Problem

Standard RNNs maintain a hidden state that is updated at each timestep by combining the current input with the previous hidden state through a shared, learned weight matrix followed by a saturating nonlinearity. Because the same recurrence is applied at every step, the network can ingest sequences of any length, and the hidden state nominally provides a running summary of all past inputs, enabling long-range temporal dependencies to be modelled with a fixed number of parameters. In practice, however, Bengio et al. [25] demonstrated that this promise is undermined by the vanishing (and exploding) gradient problem during backpropagation through time (BPTT). Unrolling the recurrence turns it into a very deep feed-forward network whose layers share weights, so the gradient of the loss with respect to an early timestep is a product of many Jacobians of the recurrent map; when the dominant factor of that product is consistently below one the gradient decays exponentially in the number of steps and vanishes, and when it is above one the gradient explodes. The exploding case can be contained by gradient clipping, but the vanishing case is more insidious because it silently prevents the optimiser from ever assigning credit across long lags, making it effectively impossible to learn dependencies spanning more than a few dozen steps. For video sampled at typical rates this confines a vanilla RNN to a horizon of barely a second or two, a fundamental restriction that motivated the gated architectures designed expressly to keep gradients alive over long sequences.

### 3.4.2 Long Short-Term Memory Networks

Hochreiter and Schmidhuber [26] addressed the vanishing gradient problem with the Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) architecture, which introduced a cell state — a separate memory channel that could carry information across many timesteps without the multiplicative degradation that afflicts the hidden state of a vanilla RNN. Three learned gating mechanisms, each a sigmoid unit reading the current input and previous hidden state, regulate information flow: the input gate controls how much new candidate information is written to the cell state; the forget gate controls how much of the existing cell state is retained; and the output gate controls how much of the cell state is exposed as the hidden state output. The decisive property is the form of the cell update, which combines the retained old state and the gated new content by addition rather than by repeated matrix multiplication and squashing; this near-linear "constant error carousel" gives the gradient a path along the cell state whose local derivative is governed by the forget gate rather than by a saturating nonlinearity, so error signals can propagate over hundreds of timesteps without vanishing and the network can learn dependencies that are simply inaccessible to a vanilla RNN. The forget gate itself was not in the original 1997 formulation but was added by Gers et al. [27] as a refinement; by allowing the network to learn when to reset its memory, it proved particularly important for continual prediction on long or variable-length sequences, where an un-resettable cell state eventually saturates. These properties made LSTMs the standard sequence model in natural language processing through the 2010s, and they were quickly adopted for video recognition as the temporal half of the CNN-plus-RNN pipelines discussed below.

Schuster and Paliwal [28] introduced Bidirectional RNNs, and by extension Bidirectional LSTMs, which process the sequence in both temporal directions with two independent recurrent layers and concatenate their hidden states at each timestep, so that the representation at any frame is informed by both the frames before it and those after it. The construction presupposes that the entire sequence is available before any output is produced, which makes it inapplicable to online, streaming prediction but a natural fit for offline action recognition, where a trimmed clip is classified in its entirety after the fact. In that setting the backward pass supplies future context that a causal model lacks — the end of a gesture can disambiguate its beginning — and bidirectional processing therefore tends to improve classification accuracy, at the cost of roughly doubling the recurrent computation and forfeiting any real-time capability. This thesis adopts exactly such an offline, bidirectional configuration for its recurrent baseline, as detailed in Section 3.4.3.

Cho et al. [29] proposed the Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) as a streamlined alternative to the LSTM that merges the cell state and hidden state into a single vector and reduces the three LSTM gates to two — an update gate that interpolates between the previous state and a new candidate, and a reset gate that modulates how much past state feeds the candidate. By dispensing with a separate output gate and an independent memory cell, the GRU has fewer parameters and fewer elementwise operations per step than an LSTM of the same width, so it trains and runs faster and is less prone to overfitting on small datasets, while empirically matching LSTM accuracy on many sequence tasks. It thus represents a favourable accuracy-efficiency trade-off and is a common default when recurrent capacity must be spent sparingly, although on tasks demanding the very longest memory the LSTM's dedicated cell state retains a modest edge.

### 3.4.3 LRCN: Long-term Recurrent Convolutional Networks

Donahue et al. [30] proposed the Long-term Recurrent Convolutional Network (LRCN), which combined deep CNNs for per-frame visual feature extraction with LSTMs for temporal sequence modelling, giving the recurrent line of work a concrete and general architecture for visual sequences. In the LRCN framework each video frame is passed through a CNN — in the original work an AlexNet- or VGGNet-style backbone [16] — to produce a fixed-length feature vector; the sequence of these vectors is then consumed by one or more LSTM layers, and a softmax over the LSTM outputs (averaged across timesteps for classification) yields the prediction. A signal strength of the design is its generality: because the recurrent half is decoupled from the visual encoder and can emit a variable-length output as readily as a single label, the same template extends beyond activity recognition to sequence-to-sequence problems such as image captioning and video description, which the authors demonstrated within one unified model. On action recognition LRCN provided an interpretable factorisation of the problem into spatial appearance modelling by the CNN and temporal dynamics modelling by the LSTM; consistent with the broader pattern seen throughout this chapter, its accuracy depended heavily on the input modality, reaching about 71% on UCF101 from RGB frames alone and around 82.9% when the frames were replaced by stacked optical flow, with a late fusion of the two modalities higher still. The LRCN paradigm serves as the CNN-RNN baseline in this thesis, instantiated with a pre-trained ResNet-50 as the frame encoder feeding a bidirectional LSTM for temporal sequence classification; the upgrade from the original shallow backbones to a residual encoder, and from a unidirectional to a bidirectional recurrence, modernises the design while preserving the two-stage CNN-then-LSTM structure that defines the family.

### 3.4.4 Limitations of RNN-Based Video Models

Despite their theoretical appeal for sequence modelling, RNN-based approaches for video recognition carry significant practical limitations, several of which proved decisive in the field's eventual move away from recurrence. First, the recurrence is inherently sequential: the hidden state at each step depends on the previous one, so the timesteps of a sequence cannot be processed in parallel during either the forward or the backward pass, and training therefore fails to exploit the massive data-parallelism of modern accelerators in the way a feed-forward CNN of comparable capacity does, yielding substantially longer wall-clock training times. Second, although the gated cell of an LSTM mitigates the vanishing-gradient problem relative to a vanilla RNN, it does not eliminate it; over hundreds of frames the forget-gated memory still attenuates and very long-range dependencies remain hard to capture, so the practical temporal horizon, while far longer than that of Section 3.4.1, is still bounded. Third, the prevailing recipe paired a separately pre-trained CNN feature extractor with an LSTM trained on top of its outputs, and in this two-stage arrangement the visual features are not in general optimised for the downstream temporal task; even when the two stages are fine-tuned together, the encoder and the recurrence are rarely balanced to equal effect, leaving accuracy on the table. Fourth, and most fundamentally, the entire past of the sequence must be compressed into a single fixed-width hidden state, an information bottleneck that cannot scale with sequence length and that struggles to retain the detail of long or complex videos. Taken together these constraints — the absence of temporal parallelism above all — motivated both the spatiotemporal convolutional architectures of Section 3.5 and, ultimately, the attention-based Transformer models of Section 3.6, whose self-attention [39] relates all timesteps directly and in parallel, dispensing with the sequential bottleneck that defines the recurrent paradigm.

## 3.5 Three-Dimensional Convolutional Neural Networks for Video

The most conceptually direct extension of image CNNs to video is to extend the two-dimensional convolution kernel to three dimensions, adding a temporal axis. Where a 2D kernel slides over height and width and aggregates appearance within a single frame, a 3D kernel of extent $t \times h \times w$ slides over height, width, and time simultaneously, so that a single weight tensor learns to respond to short spatiotemporal patterns — an edge that translates, a limb that rotates, a texture that flickers. This approach, known as the 3D CNN, jointly captures spatial appearance and temporal motion within the convolutional operation itself, without requiring separate optical flow estimation (as in the two-stream family [21]) or external sequential processing (as in the recurrent LRCN model [30]). The appeal is architectural parsimony: motion is not bolted on as a second modality but is learned end-to-end as one more dimension of the receptive field. The cost, as the remainder of this section makes clear, is that parameters, memory, and floating-point operations all grow with the additional temporal kernel dimension, and that the resulting models are data-hungry to a degree that only became tractable once large video corpora such as Kinetics-400 [34], [55] existed.

### 3.5.1 Early 3D CNNs

Ji et al. [31] and Baccouche et al. [32] independently explored 3D convolutions for action recognition in the early 2010s, several years before the deep learning era of video understanding matured. Ji et al. [31] applied 3D convolutions to short stacks of consecutive video frames for human action recognition in surveillance footage, hardwiring several channels (gradients, optical-flow components) at the input and convolving over them in space and time to produce spatiotemporal feature maps; this demonstrated, for the first time within a learned convolutional framework, that motion-sensitive features could be extracted directly rather than hand-engineered as in the space-time interest point [5] and dense-trajectory [3], [4] traditions. Baccouche et al. [32] combined a 3D convolutional front end with a recurrent LSTM [26] stage that aggregated the per-clip descriptors over longer horizons, prefiguring the later split between convolutional motion encoding and recurrent temporal reasoning. However, these early systems were severely constrained by the resources of their era: they operated on small, low-resolution inputs, used only a handful of feature maps, and relied on shallow architectures of a few layers, which together limited their representational capacity to short, coarse motion patterns. Decisively, no large-scale labelled video dataset existed to pre-train them — there was as yet no video analogue of ImageNet [13] — so the networks had to be trained from scratch on the small target datasets (on the order of the few thousand clips in KTH [51] or Weizmann [52]), which is precisely the regime in which deep models overfit. As a result their reported accuracies, while validating the concept, did not yet surpass the strongest hand-crafted pipelines such as improved dense trajectories with Fisher-vector encoding [4], [8]. The lesson carried forward was that the 3D-convolutional idea was sound but starved of data and depth.

### 3.5.2 C3D

Tran et al. [33] provided the first convincing demonstration of the power of deep 3D CNNs with C3D (Convolutional 3D), a VGG-like architecture [16] that stacked eight convolutional layers using uniform $3 \times 3 \times 3$ convolution kernels throughout, interleaved with pooling and followed by two fully connected layers, pre-trained on the large Sports-1M dataset of roughly one million sports videos. The choice of homogeneous $3 \times 3 \times 3$ kernels was not arbitrary but the outcome of a controlled study in which the authors varied the temporal kernel depth — comparing constant depths of 1, 3, 5, and 7 and increasing/decreasing schedules — and found the small, constant $3 \times 3 \times 3$ kernel to be the best-performing and most economical choice, mirroring the homogeneous $3 \times 3$ design philosophy that VGG had established for images [16]. Operating on 16-frame clips, C3D learned generic clip-level descriptors that, once the network was treated as a fixed feature extractor, could be fed to a simple linear classifier and transferred across tasks; this off-the-shelf usability, together with the public release of pre-trained weights, made C3D the standard 3D-CNN baseline of its time and a popular general-purpose video feature, reaching roughly 85.2% on UCF101 [54]. However, C3D suffered from two notable limitations that framed the agenda for everything that followed. First, its parameter count of approximately 78 million — dominated by the wide fully connected layers — made both training and inference expensive in memory and computation. Second, the 16-frame input window meant that only about half a second of motion was visible to the network at once, so genuinely long-range temporal structure was inaccessible and had to be approximated by averaging predictions over many clips. Compounding these, pre-training on Sports-1M — a weakly labelled, sports-dominated, and comparatively noisy corpus — produced features biased towards a narrow slice of human activity, limiting their generalisability to the broader action vocabularies of UCF101 [54] and HMDB51 [53]. These three weaknesses — size, short temporal horizon, and a suboptimal pre-training source — are precisely what the next architecture set out to remedy.

### 3.5.3 I3D: Inflated 3D ConvNets

The most influential 3D CNN architecture of the deep learning era was the Two-Stream Inflated 3D ConvNet (I3D) proposed by Carreira and Zisserman [34], which introduced two innovations that jointly reset the field's baseline. First, I3D proposed "inflating" a pre-trained 2D CNN — specifically the Inception-v1 / GoogLeNet design [17] with its batch-normalised Inception modules [18] — into 3D by taking each learned 2D filter of size $h \times w$, replicating it $N$ times along a new temporal axis to form an $N \times h \times w$ kernel, and dividing the replicated weights by $N$ so that the response to a constant ("boring") video equals the original 2D response on the corresponding still image. This bootstrapping-of-motion-from-stillness preserved the full benefit of ImageNet [13] pre-training: rather than initialising a 3D network randomly and learning everything from video alone, I3D began from a strong appearance model and only had to learn the temporal refinements, which dramatically eased optimisation and improved final accuracy. Pooling and stride choices were likewise adapted so that the temporal dimension was not collapsed too early. Second, and of comparable long-term importance, Carreira and Zisserman introduced the Kinetics-400 dataset [34], [55] alongside the model — a large-scale collection of 400 human action classes with roughly 240,000 trimmed training clips of about ten seconds each, sourced from YouTube and spanning a far broader and more balanced action vocabulary than Sports-1M. Kinetics rapidly superseded Sports-1M as the standard pre-training corpus for video recognition, and the two-stage recipe of pre-training on Kinetics and then fine-tuning on the smaller UCF101 [54] or HMDB51 [53] benchmarks became the canonical transfer-learning pipeline for the next several years. The empirical payoff was decisive: the two-stream variant of I3D — combining an RGB stream with an optical-flow stream [9], [10], each an inflated 3D Inception network, pre-trained on Kinetics and then fine-tuned — reached 98.0% on UCF101 and 80.9% on HMDB51 [34]. These figures advanced the state of the art by a wide margin and established a high bar against which essentially every subsequent video architecture was benchmarked; just as importantly, the paper's central message that scale of pre-training data was now the dominant factor reoriented the community towards large datasets and transfer learning.

### 3.5.4 Pseudo-3D and Factorised Approaches

The steep computational and parametric cost of full 3D convolutions — each $3 \times 3 \times 3$ kernel carries 27 weights per input–output channel pair, threefold more than the $3 \times 3$ image equivalent — motivated a line of work seeking to approximate the spatiotemporal kernel by a composition of cheaper lower-dimensional ones. Qiu et al. [35] proposed the Pseudo-3D (P3D) network, which decomposed each $3 \times 3 \times 3$ convolution into a $1 \times 3 \times 3$ spatial convolution that operates within each frame and a $3 \times 1 \times 1$ temporal convolution that operates across frames, and embedded this decomposition inside a deep ResNet-style backbone. Rather than committing to a single wiring, P3D defined three complementary residual block variants — placing the spatial and temporal convolutions in series, in parallel, or in a hybrid that feeds the spatial output forward while also summing the temporal branch — and interleaved these block types throughout the network to diversify the spatiotemporal feature transformations and to exploit the residual connections of the ResNet design for stable gradient flow. By replacing the $27$-weight cube with a $9 + 3 = 12$-weight pair per channel relationship, P3D substantially reduced parameters and floating-point operations relative to a comparable full-3D ResNet, while its much greater depth (a P3D-ResNet of 199 layers) allowed it to recover and even exceed the representational power lost to factorisation, reaching roughly 88.6% on UCF101. P3D thus established the central thesis of the factorised-convolution programme — that separating the spatial and temporal extents of the kernel buys efficiency at little or no cost in accuracy — which the next architecture would study systematically and push further.

### 3.5.5 R(2+1)D: Factorised Spatiotemporal Convolutions

Tran et al. [36] conducted a systematic, controlled empirical study of spatiotemporal convolution designs — comparing pure 2D convolution over frames, 2D convolution over clips, full 3D convolution, a mixed model that is 3D in early layers and 2D in later ones, and the factorised form — within a single ResNet-18/34 backbone, and from this comparison distilled R(2+1)D as the preferred design. The R(2+1)D block decomposes each full 3D convolution into a 2D spatial convolution followed immediately by a 1D temporal convolution, applied sequentially, where crucially the number of intermediate channels $M_i$ between the two sub-convolutions is chosen so that the factorised block matches the parameter count of the full 3D block it replaces, making the comparison fair. Two arguments explain why this factorisation is not merely cheaper but actually better. First, it is strictly more expressive at equal parameter budget, because the additional ReLU non-linearity [14] inserted between the spatial and temporal sub-convolutions doubles the number of rectifications per block and therefore the complexity of the function the block can represent — a full 3D convolution applies one non-linearity where R(2+1)D applies two. Second, the decomposition eases optimisation: by separating the spatial and temporal optimisation signals into two simpler sub-problems, it yields markedly lower training error than an otherwise identical full-3D network of the same depth and capacity, indicating that the factorised function is easier for stochastic gradient descent to fit rather than merely better regularised. The practical consequence is faster convergence and slightly higher final accuracy than equivalent full-3D architectures. Pre-trained on Kinetics-400, R(2+1)D reached 96.8% on UCF101 and 74.5% on HMDB51, with a Kinetics-400 top-1 accuracy in the region of 74–75% [36]. The R(2+1)D-18 architecture — an 18-layer ResNet-style backbone with (2+1)D convolutions throughout, initialised from Kinetics-400 pre-training — is employed in this thesis as the 3D-CNN representative. It is a deliberate choice: R(2+1)D-18 is compact enough to fine-tune under the compute budget of this work, yet it embodies the mature factorised-spatiotemporal paradigm, allowing the experiments to isolate the effect of explicit, learned spatiotemporal convolution against both the 2D-CNN appearance baseline and the attention-based Transformer baseline introduced in Section 3.6.

### 3.5.6 SlowFast Networks and X3D

Feichtenhofer et al. [37] proposed SlowFast Networks, which processed the same video through two parallel pathways operating at deliberately different temporal resolutions, by analogy with the magnocellular and parvocellular streams of biological vision. A Slow pathway sampled frames at a low frame rate (large temporal stride) but carried high channel capacity, devoting most of the model's parameters and computation to rich semantic and spatial representation; a Fast pathway sampled the same clip at several times the frame rate (for instance an $\alpha = 8$ ratio) but was kept deliberately lightweight, using only a small fraction (around an eighth) of the channels so that, despite its dense temporal sampling, it consumed only a modest share of the total floating-point operations. Time-strided lateral connections fused the Fast pathway's fine-grained motion features into the Slow pathway at multiple stages, so that high-frame-rate motion cues could inform the high-capacity semantic stream without the latter having to be evaluated densely in time. This explicit decoupling of "what" (slow, high-capacity, appearance) from "how it moves" (fast, low-capacity, motion) proved that dual-rate temporal modelling supplies complementary information that neither pathway captures alone, and the design reached roughly 79.8% top-1 on Kinetics-400 in its strong R101 + non-local, $8 \times 8$ configuration [37], together with state-of-the-art results on the spatiotemporal-detection benchmark AVA [61]. Feichtenhofer [38] subsequently proposed X3D, which approached efficiency from the opposite direction: instead of pruning a large network, X3D began from a tiny 2D image-classification network and progressively expanded it along a small set of axes — temporal duration (clip length), frame rate (temporal sampling density), spatial resolution, network width (channels), and depth (layers) — one axis at a time, each step guided by a simple efficiency–accuracy criterion that retained the expansion giving the best accuracy per unit of added computation. This coordinate-style search yielded a family of models spanning a wide range of operating points; notably, the larger X3D-XL matched the accuracy of SlowFast at roughly five times fewer floating-point operations, reaching about 79.1% on Kinetics-400 [38], thereby pushing the accuracy–efficiency Pareto frontier of video recognition substantially outward. Together, SlowFast and X3D mark the point at which the 3D-convolution programme turned its attention decisively from raw accuracy towards the accuracy-versus-cost trade-off that is the central concern of this thesis.

### 3.5.7 Limitations of 3D CNNs

Despite their strong performance, 3D CNN architectures carry inherent limitations that shaped the field's pivot towards attention. Most fundamentally, the computational cost of 3D convolution grows steeply with the spatiotemporal kernel: enlarging the temporal extent multiplies the operations of every convolutional layer, so that genuinely large spatiotemporal receptive fields are expensive, and this is the very pressure that motivated the factorised P3D [35], R(2+1)D [36], and X3D [38] designs above. The increased parameter counts also make the models data-hungry — training from scratch demands very large video corpora, and even with Kinetics-400 pre-training [34], fine-tuning on the comparatively small UCF101 [54] and HMDB51 [53] benchmarks risks overfitting and typically requires careful regularisation [15] and augmentation. Most consequential for modelling, the receptive field of a convolutional stack is local and fixed: any single layer sees only its small kernel neighbourhood, so dependencies between events separated by many seconds can be represented only indirectly, by stacking many layers until their composed receptive field finally spans the gap. This is an inefficient and indirect route to long-range temporal reasoning, and it contrasts sharply with a mechanism that can relate any two positions in a single step. That contrast is exactly what the self-attention operation provides, and it is the conceptual bridge from this section to the Transformer architectures of Section 3.6.

## 3.6 Attention Mechanisms and Transformers

The introduction of the Transformer architecture [39] and its subsequent extension to visual domains represents perhaps the most significant architectural shift in deep learning since the advent of residual connections. By replacing the local, fixed receptive field of convolution with a content-based attention operation that can relate any two elements of its input directly, the Transformer addresses precisely the long-range-dependency limitation that closed Section 3.5. Transformer-based models have since progressively supplanted or augmented CNN-based approaches across virtually every computer vision task, including action recognition, and the trajectory traced in this section — from sequence attention to the Vision Transformer to space-time video Transformers and finally to masked-autoencoder pre-training — culminates in VideoMAE [45], the Transformer model adopted in this thesis.

### 3.6.1 Attention Mechanisms in Sequence Models

Bahdanau et al. [40] introduced soft attention for neural machine translation, addressing a bottleneck in the then-standard encoder–decoder recurrent model: compressing an entire variable-length source sentence into a single fixed-length context vector, which degraded badly as sentences grew long. Their solution let the decoder, at each output step, attend selectively across all of the encoder's hidden states rather than relying on one summary vector. Concretely, the model computed an alignment score between the current decoder state and every encoder state, normalised these scores into a probability distribution, and formed the context as the corresponding weighted sum of encoder states, so that the network learned softly which source words to focus on when emitting each target word. This produced large gains in translation quality on long sentences and, just as importantly, yielded interpretable alignment maps, establishing attention as a general-purpose mechanism for selectively routing information through a neural network. The idea that the relevant context for a prediction should be gathered by learned, content-dependent weighting — rather than fixed by architecture — is the seed from which self-attention and the Transformer grew.

### 3.6.2 The Transformer Architecture

Vaswani et al. [39] proposed the Transformer, which took the attention idea to its logical conclusion by eliminating recurrence and convolution entirely and making self-attention the sole sequence-mixing primitive. In self-attention, each position projects its representation into a query, a key, and a value vector; the affinity between positions $i$ and $j$ is the dot product of query $i$ with key $j$, scaled by $1/\sqrt{d_k}$ — the scaled dot-product attention $\mathrm{softmax}(QK^{\top}/\sqrt{d_k})V$, where the $\sqrt{d_k}$ divisor counteracts the growth of dot-product magnitudes in high dimension that would otherwise saturate the softmax and starve gradients — and each output is the resulting attention-weighted sum of value vectors. Multi-Head Attention runs several such attention functions in parallel in distinct learned subspaces and concatenates their outputs, letting the model attend simultaneously to different kinds of relationship (for instance syntactic and semantic, or near and far). The property that makes this so powerful for long-range modelling is that self-attention provides a constant path length of one between any two positions, however far apart — whereas in a recurrent network the path length grows linearly with their separation, attenuating the gradient and making distant dependencies hard to learn [25] — so every pair of tokens enjoys a direct gradient route. Because self-attention is permutation-equivariant and therefore blind to order on its own, the Transformer adds positional encodings to the token representations to reinject sequence position, and wraps each attention and feed-forward sub-layer in residual connections and layer normalisation for trainable depth. These models were then pre-trained at scale with self-supervised objectives — most influentially the masked-language-modelling of BERT [41], which masks a fraction of input tokens and predicts them from bidirectional context — and exhibited remarkable transfer to downstream tasks, inaugurating the modern era of foundation models and, crucially for what follows, demonstrating that masked-token reconstruction is an effective pre-training signal.

### 3.6.3 Vision Transformer (ViT)

Dosovitskiy et al. [42] demonstrated that the Transformer encoder could be applied to images almost unchanged, dispensing with the convolutional inductive biases of locality and translation equivariance, provided that sufficient pre-training data were available to compensate. In ViT, the input image is partitioned into a grid of fixed-size patches — typically $16 \times 16$ pixels, so that a $224 \times 224$ image yields $14 \times 14 = 196$ patches — and each patch is flattened and linearly projected into a token embedding; a learnable classification token is prepended and positional embeddings are added, after which the sequence is processed by a standard multi-head self-attention encoder identical in form to the language Transformer [39]. The headline result was that, when pre-trained on the very large proprietary JFT-300M corpus of around 300 million labelled images and then transferred, ViT matched or surpassed the strongest contemporary CNNs on ImageNet [13] while costing substantially less to pre-train, with the largest ViT-H/14 model attaining roughly 88.55% top-1 on ImageNet. Equally instructive was the failure mode: trained only at ImageNet scale, ViT underperformed comparable CNNs, because without convolution's built-in priors the model must learn locality and translation structure from data, and there is not enough of it at that scale — a clean empirical illustration of the inductive-bias-versus-data trade-off. By fixing patches-as-tokens as the standard interface between pixels and a Transformer, ViT supplied the exact representational template that the video architectures below adopt, the only open question being how to extend the patch tokenisation and the attention pattern across time.

### 3.6.4 TimeSformer

Bertasius, Wang, and Torresani [43] proposed TimeSformer (Time-Space Transformer), which extended ViT directly to video and in doing so confronted the central obstacle of attention on video: sequence length. Tokenising a clip of $T$ frames into $16 \times 16$ patches from $224 \times 224$ inputs yields $T \times 196$ tokens — already $1{,}568$ tokens at a modest $T = 8$ — and because the cost of full self-attention is quadratic in the number of tokens, jointly attending over all space and time becomes prohibitive in both memory and computation. TimeSformer's remedy was divided (factorised) space-time attention: within each block it applies temporal attention first, letting each token attend only to the tokens at the same spatial location in the other frames, and then spatial attention, letting each token attend only to the tokens within its own frame, so that the two attention operations compose to cover spatiotemporal context without ever materialising the full joint attention matrix. This factorisation lowers the dominant attention cost from the joint $O(T^2 H^2 W^2)$ to the additive $O(T^2 + H^2 W^2)$, rendering the model tractable while still propagating information along both axes across stacked blocks. The authors found this divided scheme to give the best accuracy–efficiency balance among several space-time attention variants, and TimeSformer reached about 78.0% on Kinetics-400 in its base configuration and roughly 80.7% in the larger, higher-resolution, longer-clip TimeSformer-L variant [43]. Its significance was conceptual as much as numerical: a convolution-free, pure-attention model could match strong 3D-CNN baselines on the standard benchmark, confirming that the limitations catalogued in Section 3.5.7 were not intrinsic to the video task and that attention was a viable backbone for it.

### 3.6.5 Video Swin Transformer

Liu et al. [44] proposed the Video Swin Transformer, which carried the hierarchical, locally windowed design of the image Swin Transformer into the spatiotemporal domain as an alternative way to tame attention's quadratic cost. Instead of attending globally, Swin computes self-attention only within non-overlapping local windows, which makes the per-layer cost grow linearly rather than quadratically with the number of tokens; to restore the cross-window information flow that strictly local windows would forfeit, alternate layers shift the window partition by half a window so that tokens which were separated in one layer share a window in the next. In the video variant the windows are genuine 3D spatiotemporal volumes — for instance $8 \times 7 \times 7$ patches spanning time, height, and width — and the shifting is applied jointly across all three axes, so adjacent 3D windows progressively exchange information as depth increases, while a hierarchical patch-merging stage builds a multi-scale feature pyramid much as a CNN does. This combination of linear-complexity windowed attention with a hierarchical, locality-respecting structure reintroduces a measure of the inductive bias that pure ViT-style models discard, which improves data efficiency. Initialised from ImageNet-21K pre-training, the large Video Swin-L model reached about 84.9% on Kinetics-400 [44], surpassing TimeSformer and establishing the shifted-window Transformer as a leading video architecture — and, in doing so, sharpening the open problem that the next method addresses: the strongest video Transformers still depended on costly large-scale supervised image or video pre-training.

### 3.6.6 VideoMAE: Masked Autoencoding for Video

The dominant route to initialising large Transformer-based video models — exemplified by Video Swin's reliance on ImageNet-21K supervision [44] — required expensive supervised pre-training on millions of labelled images or video clips, a dependency that VideoMAE was designed to remove. Tong et al. [45] adapted the Masked Autoencoder (MAE) framework of He et al. [46] to video to obtain a data-efficient, self-supervised pre-training scheme. The MAE recipe for images masks a large fraction of patches and trains an asymmetric encoder–decoder to reconstruct the missing pixels: the encoder processes only the small set of visible patches, and a lightweight decoder then reconstructs the full image from the encoded visible patches plus shared, learnable mask tokens — a design that, at a 75% masking ratio, lets a ViT-H reach about 87.8% on ImageNet [46] while making pre-training cheap because the heavy encoder never sees the masked majority. VideoMAE inherits this asymmetry but pushes the masking far harder, masking an extreme 90% of spatiotemporal patch tokens, and does so with tube masking — the same spatial mask is applied to every frame so that a masked location stays masked along its entire temporal extent. Both choices follow from the defining property of video, temporal redundancy: because consecutive frames are highly correlated, a frame-independent or low masking ratio would let the network reconstruct a hidden patch by simply copying its visible counterpart in a neighbouring frame, a trivial temporal interpolation that teaches little; the 90% tube mask closes this shortcut and forces the encoder to infer the missing content from genuinely distant spatiotemporal context, learning real motion and structure rather than copying. The efficiency consequence is striking: with 90% of tokens removed the encoder runs on only about 10% of the patches, so the most expensive component is evaluated on a tenth of the sequence, and the full token set — visible encodings interleaved with mask tokens — is reassembled only in the shallow decoder used for reconstruction. After this self-supervised pre-training and subsequent supervised fine-tuning on Kinetics-400, VideoMAE attains roughly 81.5% top-1 with a ViT-Base backbone, about 85.2% with ViT-Large, and about 86.6% with ViT-Huge [45], competitive with or exceeding heavier architectures trained under fully supervised objectives on the same data. Decisively for resource-constrained settings, the self-supervised objective also confers strong data efficiency: VideoMAE can be pre-trained effectively on the target domain's own unlabelled clips — even datasets as small as UCF101 [54] or HMDB51 [53] — rather than requiring a massive external labelled corpus, loosening exactly the data dependency that earlier video Transformers suffered from. For these reasons the VideoMAE ViT-Base model, fine-tuned from Kinetics-400 pre-training, serves as the Transformer baseline in this thesis: it is a compact yet faithful representative of the modern masked-autoencoding, attention-based paradigm, and it sits at a parameter and compute scale commensurate with the R(2+1)D-18 and 2D-CNN baselines, enabling a controlled comparison of accuracy against efficiency across the three architectural families.

### 3.6.7 Self-Supervised and Foundation Model Approaches

Beyond VideoMAE, a rich body of work has explored self-supervised pre-training for video representations, broadly along two axes. Contrastive methods adapted image frameworks such as SimCLR [47], MoCo [48], and the self-distillation approach DINO [49] to the temporal domain, typically treating different temporal crops or differently augmented views of the same clip as positive pairs to be pulled together in embedding space while distinct videos are pushed apart, thereby learning representations invariant to nuisance transformations without labels; these stand as the contrastive counterpart to VideoMAE's generative masked-reconstruction objective. Along the other axis, CLIP [50] showed that contrastive image–text pre-training on web-scale image–caption data yields representations that transfer remarkably well to video recognition under zero-shot or few-shot evaluation, simply by matching frame embeddings against natural-language class descriptions, which catalysed a wave of video–language pre-training that grounds visual representations in text. These foundation-model approaches define the current frontier of the field and increasingly set the state of the art, but they depend on data and compute resources well beyond the budget of this work, and so they inform the context of this thesis without being candidates for direct empirical comparison within it.

## 3.7 Benchmark Datasets for Action Recognition

The progression of action recognition performance has been inextricably linked to the availability of increasingly large, diverse, and challenging benchmark datasets. Each new dataset has exposed limitations of existing methods and driven architectural innovation, and the field's history can be read almost directly from the sequence of benchmarks that successive generations of models were forced to confront. Early controlled corpora measured only whether a method could discriminate a handful of staged movements; later web-scale collections introduced the cluttered backgrounds, viewpoint changes, motion blur, and label noise of unconstrained video, while the most recent diagnostic benchmarks were engineered specifically to penalise models that classify from static appearance alone. A recurring pattern is that a benchmark stimulates progress until it saturates, at which point reported accuracies cluster near the ceiling and cease to separate competing methods, prompting the community to construct a harder successor. Understanding the scale, sourcing, and evaluation protocol of each dataset is therefore essential both for interpreting the numbers reported in the literature and for justifying the choice of benchmarks used in this thesis.

### 3.7.1 Early Benchmarks

The KTH dataset [51], introduced by Schuldt et al. in 2004, provided one of the first standardised benchmarks for action recognition. It consists of 2,391 video sequences of six simple actions — walking, jogging, running, boxing, hand-waving, and hand-clapping — performed by 25 subjects across four recording scenarios (outdoors, outdoors with scale variation, outdoors with different clothing, and indoors), filmed against largely homogeneous backgrounds with a static camera. While KTH enabled the systematic, quantitative comparison of early space-time interest-point and local-feature methods, its small scale, six-word action vocabulary, single-actor framing, and tightly controlled conditions meant that saturating its accuracy did not predict real-world performance: multiple early hand-crafted approaches exceeded 90% mean accuracy, after which the benchmark could no longer discriminate between competing pipelines. The Weizmann dataset [52], released by Blank et al. in 2005, was smaller still, comprising roughly 90 clips spanning 10 actions performed by a handful of subjects, with foreground silhouettes that were clean enough to support "actions as space-time shapes" representations. Like KTH it was valuable for algorithm development and rapid prototyping but, with so few clips and such constrained appearance, it was wholly insufficient for evaluating the generalisation that real deployment demands. Together these two corpora established the experimental conventions of the field — fixed action vocabularies, per-subject or per-split evaluation, mean accuracy as the headline metric — even as their saturation made plain that progress would require benchmarks drawn from genuinely unconstrained video.

### 3.7.2 HMDB51

Kuehne et al. [53] introduced HMDB51 (Human Motion DataBase) in 2011, collecting 6,766 video clips across 51 action categories drawn from diverse real-world sources including feature films, public web repositories, and YouTube, with at least 101 clips guaranteed per category to ensure that every class was adequately populated. Three official train/test splits were provided, each allocating 70 training and 30 test clips per class, with performance conventionally reported as the mean accuracy across all three splits so that results are not dependent on a single fortunate partition. HMDB51 was, and remains, substantially more challenging than UCF101 for two compounding reasons. First, inter-class similarity is high: many action pairs such as "climb" and "climb stairs", or "run" and "jog", share almost identical visual appearance and differ chiefly in subtle motion cues, so that a model relying on coarse scene context is easily misled. Second, within-class variability is severe, because the heterogeneous source material introduces wide variation in camera motion, viewpoint, illumination, resolution, and the presence of cinematic editing, meaning that two instances of the same label may look nothing alike. The drawing of clips from professionally shot films also injects systematic camera movement and shot cuts that are absent from the staged early benchmarks. These properties make HMDB51 an effective discriminator of model quality precisely where it matters — the modelling of fine-grained motion rather than gross appearance — and it is for this reason that HMDB51 is adopted as one of the two primary evaluation benchmarks in this thesis, providing a deliberately difficult setting in which the four architectures under study can be meaningfully separated.

### 3.7.3 UCF101

Soomro et al. [54] introduced UCF101 in 2012, extending the earlier UCF50 dataset to 101 action classes comprising 13,320 trimmed clips collected from YouTube and totalling approximately 27 hours of video. The classes are organised into five major groups — human-object interaction, body-motion only, human-human interaction, playing musical instruments, and sports — a taxonomy that deliberately spans both fine-grained manipulations and whole-body activities. As with HMDB51, three official train/test splits are provided and accuracy is averaged across them to obtain a stable estimate. UCF101 became the most widely cited action recognition benchmark of the 2012–2018 period owing to its then-substantial scale, its clear and well-separated class definitions, and the convenience of pre-segmented, trimmed clips that reduced evaluation to clip-level classification and so simplified experimentation. Its sustained popularity also produced a dense web of comparable published results, which made it the default proving ground on which each new architectural idea was expected to demonstrate an improvement. Although it is appreciably easier than HMDB51 — reported accuracies for strong modern models sit well above those on HMDB51, and the benchmark is now close to saturation for heavily pretrained networks — its diversity and familiarity keep it indispensable for situating new work against the historical record. UCF101 is used as the second evaluation benchmark in this thesis, complementing HMDB51 by offering a somewhat easier yet more varied test of recognition accuracy, so that the two datasets together bracket a useful range of difficulty.

### 3.7.4 Kinetics

Kay et al. [55] introduced Kinetics-400 in 2017, a dataset of approximately 240,000 ten-second YouTube training clips across 400 action classes (around 306,000 clips in total once the validation and held-out test partitions are included), with a separate validation set of roughly 50 clips per class and an undisclosed test set used for challenge evaluation. By fixing clip length at about ten seconds and curating one labelled action per clip, Kinetics was both far larger and far more diverse than UCF101 and HMDB51, and its scale was the decisive enabler for training high-capacity 3D CNNs and, later, Transformers either from scratch or as a strong pre-training target. The release of Kinetics-400 allowed I3D [34] to demonstrate the value of large-scale video pre-training and triggered a durable shift in the field whereby Kinetics-pretrained weights became the standard initialisation for UCF101 and HMDB51 experiments rather than a mere option. Kinetics-600 [56], released in 2018, expanded the vocabulary to 600 classes with roughly 480,000 clips, and Kinetics-700 [57] extended it further to 700 classes with on the order of 650,000 clips, each step trading additional annotation effort for broader coverage. In this thesis, both R(2+1)D and VideoMAE are initialised from Kinetics-400 pretrained weights before fine-tuning, which makes Kinetics central to the experimental design; it also means that any comparison drawn from the literature must note whether Kinetics pre-training was used, since the resulting head-start typically accounts for a large fraction of the gap between strong and weak reported numbers and can dwarf the effect of architecture alone.

### 3.7.5 Other Benchmarks

Beyond the trimmed clip-classification benchmarks, several datasets target harder formulations of the recognition problem and illuminate capabilities that UCF101 and HMDB51 cannot probe. THUMOS'14 [58], introduced by Jiang et al. in 2014, popularised untrimmed-video evaluation requiring temporal action localisation — predicting not merely which action occurs but the temporal interval in which it occurs within a long, mostly background video — substantially raising the difficulty beyond clip-level labelling. ActivityNet [59], released by Caba Heilbron et al. in 2015, scaled temporal localisation to 200 activity classes across roughly 20,000 untrimmed videos totalling approximately 849 hours, with individual videos running to several minutes and containing sparse, variable-length activity segments embedded in extended footage. Something-Something v2 [60], from Goyal et al., was designed expressly to demand temporal reasoning: it contains 220,847 short clips of humans performing fine-grained object interactions across 174 classes, where the correct label is determined by the temporal order of events — for instance "moving something from left to right" versus "moving something from right to left" are distinct classes that are indistinguishable from any single frame. Models that rely solely on appearance statistics without modelling temporal order perform poorly on this benchmark, which makes it a sharp diagnostic for genuine temporal-reasoning capability rather than appearance shortcutting. AVA [61], introduced by Gu et al. in 2018, addressed yet another axis by defining spatiotemporal person-level localisation over 80 atomic action classes, requiring a model to detect each person and label their concurrent fine-grained actions, so that several simultaneously occurring actions must be recognised within a single scene. Although none of these benchmarks is used for the controlled experiments reported here, they collectively define the broader landscape of the task and clarify why this thesis restricts its scope to trimmed clip classification on UCF101 and HMDB51: doing so isolates the architectural accuracy-versus-efficiency question from the additional confounds of localisation, untrimmed retrieval, and multi-actor detection.

## 3.8 Comparative Studies and Efficiency Analysis

Accurate assessment of a model's practical value requires not only measuring its accuracy on benchmarks but also characterising its computational cost and memory requirements, since two models with indistinguishable accuracy may differ by an order of magnitude in the floating-point operations, parameters, and latency they demand at inference. The literature contains numerous comparative studies that attempt this multi-dimensional accounting, yet direct comparison across papers is persistently complicated by inconsistent evaluation protocols, so that headline accuracy figures frequently reflect differences in input sampling and test-time augmentation as much as differences in the underlying architecture. The remainder of this section surveys the influential efficiency analyses that frame the present work, examines why fair cross-paper comparison is so difficult in the video domain, and explains how this thesis is constructed to avoid those confounds.

### 3.8.1 Efficiency Benchmarks for Image Classification

Canziani et al. [62] conducted, in 2016, a systematic empirical comparison of ImageNet image-classification architectures along multiple dimensions simultaneously, including top-1 accuracy, computational cost measured in G-Ops per inference, parameter count, memory footprint, and wall-clock inference time on a fixed GPU at several batch sizes. Their analysis revealed that many high-accuracy architectures were strikingly inefficient in practice: VGGNet, in particular, consumed disproportionate memory and operations relative to the accuracy it delivered, whereas the ResNet family occupied a favourable region of the accuracy-efficiency frontier, achieving comparable or better accuracy at substantially lower cost. Crucially, they also showed that accuracy does not increase linearly with computation — there is a regime of strongly diminishing returns in which large additional cost buys only marginal accuracy — and that operations count, rather than parameter count alone, is often the better predictor of inference time. This style of multi-dimensional benchmarking, which ranks models by where they sit on a trade-off surface rather than by accuracy in isolation, directly motivated the framing of this thesis, in which the accuracy-versus-efficiency trade-off is treated as the primary object of study rather than as an afterthought to a leaderboard position.

### 3.8.2 Video Model Efficiency Studies

Fan et al. [63] released PySlowFast in 2020, an open-source framework for video understanding accompanied by a systematic study of video-model efficiency conducted under standardised settings. Their analysis characterised the GFLOPs and parameter counts of SlowFast variants, X3D, and related architectures on a common footing, and demonstrated that X3D [38] could attain accuracy competitive with the considerably heavier SlowFast networks at a small fraction of the computational cost, reinforcing the lesson that careful architectural design can shift a model along the efficiency frontier far more effectively than brute-force scaling. By providing reference implementations and reproducible configurations, PySlowFast also did much to standardise how video-model cost is reported in subsequent work. Nevertheless, such analyses remain acutely sensitive to the number of input frames sampled per clip, the spatial resolution, and whether single-crop or multi-crop, single-clip or multi-clip evaluation is used — and because these settings vary substantially across papers, even cost figures expressed in nominally comparable units (GFLOPs, parameters) can be misleading when the underlying sampling and test-time protocols differ. The efficiency of a video model is thus not a single intrinsic number but a function of the operating point at which it is run, which makes a fixed, explicitly stated protocol indispensable for any honest comparison.

### 3.8.3 The Difficulty of Fair Comparison

A persistent and well-recognised challenge in the video recognition literature is the difficulty of performing genuinely fair comparisons across architectures. Different papers use different numbers of input frames (commonly 8, 16, 32, or 64), different spatial resolutions (typically in the range 224×224 to 320×320), different numbers of test clips and crops per video (ranging from a single clip with a single crop up to ten clips with three spatial crops, i.e. thirty forward passes aggregated per video), different pre-training datasets and pre-training durations, and different data-augmentation regimes. Each of these axes independently affects reported accuracy, and their effects compound: as a rule of thumb a factor-of-two change in frame count or in the number of test crops can move clip-classification accuracy by several percentage points, which is frequently larger than the margin separating two architectures being compared. Bertasius et al. [43] noted in the TimeSformer work that architecture comparisons made without controlling for these variables are unreliable, precisely because such protocol differences can account for swings of several points; and the closer-look study of spatiotemporal convolutions by Tran et al. [36] similarly observed that reported numbers are often not directly comparable across methods owing to these confounds. The implication is that a leaderboard ranking can invert under a change of protocol that has nothing to do with the models' intrinsic merits. This observation directly motivates the central contribution of this thesis: by training and evaluating all four architectures — ResNet-50 TSN, LRCN, R(2+1)D-18, and VideoMAE ViT-B — under a single, controlled experimental protocol with identical pre-processing, identical frame counts, and identical evaluation procedures, the accuracy-versus-efficiency trade-offs can be assessed without the confounding factors that pervade cross-paper comparison, so that any observed differences can be attributed to the architectures themselves rather than to incidental experimental choices.

### 3.8.4 The Architecture Landscape circa 2024

The progression from hand-crafted features through 2D CNNs, RNNs, 3D CNNs, and Transformers reflects a consistent trend in which each generation addressed a specific limitation of its predecessor. Hand-crafted descriptors were superseded by learned features capable of adapting to the statistics of the training data. Two-stream and TSN-style 2D CNNs processed spatial appearance efficiently and economically but relied on explicitly computed optical flow to inject motion information, paying that cost outside the network. RNNs added sequential temporal modelling on top of frame features but were constrained by inherently sequential computation and by the vanishing-gradient difficulty that limits the temporal horizon they can model. 3D CNNs jointly learned spatiotemporal features in a single homogeneous architecture but were computationally expensive and strongly dependent on large-scale pre-training to reach their potential. Transformers modelled global spatiotemporal dependencies without the spatial-locality bias of convolution, gaining flexibility at the price of even larger pre-training requirements to compensate for their weaker inductive biases. Self-supervised pre-training, exemplified by VideoMAE [45], partially relieved that data burden by learning useful representations from unlabelled video, decoupling representation quality from the cost of human annotation. Each step therefore bought a new capability while incurring a characteristic cost in computation, data, or both, and no single family dominates on every axis at once.

What remains comparatively under-explored in the literature is the systematic accuracy-versus-efficiency characterisation of representative models from each of these generations under genuinely controlled conditions on standard benchmarks. Comprehensive surveys such as that of Kong and Jing [64] provide broad and valuable coverage of the field, but they typically aggregate results reported in disparate papers — each obtained under its own frame count, resolution, crop protocol, and pre-training regime — rather than re-measuring those models under one shared experimental protocol, which leaves the very confounds discussed above unresolved. The present thesis addresses this gap directly, providing an empirical comparison of four architectures spanning three generations — 2D-CNN (ResNet-50 TSN), RNN (LRCN), 3D-CNN (R(2+1)D-18), and Transformer (VideoMAE ViT-B) — measured under identical conditions on UCF101 and HMDB51, with runtime profiling to characterise inference cost alongside accuracy, so that the resulting trade-off picture reflects the architectures themselves rather than the heterogeneous experimental choices that ordinarily separate them.
